The role of ethical theory

Is there one right answer — or many?

Before the theories, a choice about how to use them. Crane and colleagues land on the third option: pluralism. Don't pick a single winner; let each theory throw light from a different angle.

One truth, always

Absolutism

There are eternal, universal moral principles. Right and wrong are objective and can be reasoned out, whatever the circumstances. Most "Western modernist" theories lean this way.

It depends, entirely

Relativism

Morality is context-dependent and subjective — it follows the traditions and convictions of whoever is deciding. Two opposite verdicts can both be "right".

This site's stance

Pluralism

Incompatible values can each be legitimate. Pluralism neither flattens every view as equal nor crowns one — it stays open to many, and works out which matter most here.

The lens

Pick one theory, focus the dilemma to a single consideration — rights, or duty, or outcomes. Sharp, but narrow.

The prism

Pass the dilemma through every theory at once. You lose the single tidy answer — and gain the full picture. That's how this site works.

Game 02 · the main event

Refract a dilemma

Choose a case — or type your own — and send it through all ten theories. Watch one question split into a spectrum of considerations. For your own dilemma, jot a note in each band; the point isn't a verdict, it's seeing the whole.

Step 1 — choose your light source

Your spectrum reading

No single band is "the answer". A pluralist looks across all of them — notices where they agree (often a strong signal), where they clash (the real tension to resolve), and decides which considerations matter most here. That weighing is the decision.

Game 03 · justice, in miniature

Step behind the veil

Rawls asks you to design society's rules before you know who you'll be in it — the "veil of ignorance". A rule only counts as just if you'd accept it not knowing your own place. Draw a role and test your fairness.

Putting it together

A pluralist walk-through

Here's the checklist Crane offers — one question per theory — applied to a real, ordinary dilemma: a close friend asks you to lie to your manager to cover a mistake they made. No band decides alone; together they map the whole choice.

The pluralist move: outcomes, duty, and the workplace contract all point away from lying, while care reminds you not to abandon your friend. The decision that honours the most bands — help them own the mistake — is usually the one worth making.
